# Does earlier cancer detection improving five-year survival rates imply that screening programs save lives?

## Argument
A rising five-year survival rate after a screening program is introduced is weak evidence that the program saves lives, because the survival statistic is distorted by the very act of early detection. Survival is measured from the date of diagnosis. If screening moves the diagnosis earlier without changing the date of death, the measured survival interval lengthens even though the patient dies at exactly the time they otherwise would have. This is lead-time bias: the clock starts sooner, so recorded survival is longer, with no underlying change in outcome.

A second distortion pushes in the same direction. Screening preferentially catches slow-growing, indolent tumors, because they spend more time in the detectable-but-asymptomatic window where a scan can find them; fast, aggressive tumors are more likely to surface symptomatically in the gaps between screens. The screen-detected population is therefore enriched for cases with a better prognosis to begin with — length-time bias — which inflates survival regardless of whether treatment changed anyone's course. In the extreme, screening detects indolent growths that would never have caused harm at all, adding effectively cured-by-definition cases to the count.

Because both biases raise five-year survival without necessarily lowering the death rate, an improvement in survival is consistent with a program that saves many lives, a few, or none. The only way to separate these is to ask whether the screened population actually dies of the disease less often than a comparable unscreened one — disease-specific, and ideally all-cause, mortality measured against a proper control group rather than against historical survival figures. Survival rates answer a different question than the one a screening program must be justified by, so on their own they cannot settle whether it saves lives.
