# Should we expect the returns to scientific funding to diminish as a field matures?

## Argument
Returns to scientific funding must diminish as a field matures, and funders should plan around it. Early in a field's life the most consequential discoveries are also the cheapest: foundational results sit close to the surface, and a handful of researchers can reach them with modest resources. Those discoveries are made once. As the field matures the accessible questions get answered, so what remains is progressively harder — each new advance demands larger teams, costlier instruments, and longer timelines to reach ground earlier generations could not have reached at all.

The evidence is striking and consistent. Across mature fields — semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, crop yields, particle physics — research productivity, measured as discoveries or innovations per researcher, has fallen steadily for decades, even as the number of researchers and the dollars behind them rose sharply. It now takes vastly more scientific effort to sustain the same rate of progress than it did a generation ago. Ideas, in short, are getting harder to find, and no amount of funding reverses a depletion intrinsic to the activity: every answered question is one that cannot be discovered again.

The implication for funders is uncomfortable but clear. A maturing field offers less scientific return per dollar than it used to, and pouring additional money into it chases a shrinking margin. The rational response is to accept declining returns as the natural price of maturity and to shift resources toward younger fields, where the foundational fruit is still unpicked and each dollar buys more discovery. Treating a mature field's appetite for funding as evidence that the funding is well spent inverts the very lesson the productivity data are trying to teach.
